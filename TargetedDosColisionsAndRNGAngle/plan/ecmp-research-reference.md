## 1. The research idea — original state

### Core mechanism
Load balancers use a hash of the packet 5-tuple (source IP, destination IP, source
port, destination port, protocol) plus a salt value to decide which parallel link
to send traffic over. This is ECMP — Equal-Cost Multi-Path routing. It is the
default path-selection mechanism in virtually every serious network built in the
last fifteen years: data centre leaf-spine fabrics, ISP backbones, SD-WAN, Kubernetes
CNI plugins, and 5G core networks.

If the salt is predictable — generated by a weak PRNG with a guessable or
reconstructable seed — an attacker who knows or can guess the salt can craft packet
5-tuples that all hash to the same egress link. All attacker traffic lands on one
link. That link saturates. Any victim whose flows share that link loses throughput.
The rest of the fabric is untouched.

Swapping the salt source to a CSPRNG or QRNG makes the salt unguessable, the
collision set uncomputable, and the attack fails.

### Original framing
The study was originally framed as: does a predictable ECMP hash salt enable
targeted single-link flooding, and does an unpredictable salt prevent it?

Original paper strength assessment: **72/100.**

Docked for:
- Weak link to quantum — CSPRNG alone defeats the attack, QRNG adds nothing
  practically, so framing it as a quantum paper is not defensible
- The core finding "predictable salt is bad" is not novel enough on its own
- The contribution was one clean graph but not a complete argument

### Original metrics
- Maximum link utilisation
- Load variance across links (Jain's fairness index)
- Victim flow throughput under attack
- Packets and flows needed to cause congestion
- Time-to-saturation of the targeted link

### Original target venues
ACM ANCS, IEEE ICNP, IFIP Networking

## 2. Where the attack is relevant in the real world

The attack surface is not a lab curiosity. ECMP is pervasive:

- **Multi-tenant cloud fabrics** — an attacker VM and a victim VM share the same
  physical fabric. The attacker does not need controller access. If the fabric's
  ECMP salt is predictable, the attacker routes their flows onto the specific spine
  link carrying the victim's traffic. Surgical DoS, hard to distinguish from
  organic congestion.

- **Microservices / east-west traffic** — a compromised internal service or
  malicious container in a shared cluster can degrade specific service dependencies
  without touching external traffic.

- **SD-WAN branch networks** — some branch appliances run embedded software with
  weaker PRNG implementations. An attacker on the LAN side can saturate the uplink
  used by VoIP or video while leaving general internet traffic apparently functional.

- **5G core network fabric** — UPF-to-UPF paths increasingly run on commodity
  servers connected by ECMP fabrics. A compromised network function can target
  specific subscriber slices.

The most compelling venue for the paper is the **multi-tenant cloud scenario**
because: ECMP is most pervasive there, the salt-predictability assumption is most
plausible to defend, the victim has no visibility into or control over the fabric,
and a surgical low-volume attack has the most asymmetric value to an attacker.

### Why the attack is harder to detect than volume-based DoS
- The targeted link looks like organic congestion — first diagnosis is always a
  misconfigured flow, not an attack
- The attacker's traffic is individually valid — no malformed packets, no obvious
  signature, standard DDoS detection does not fire
- The attacker needs far fewer packets than a volume flood — cheap and low-profile
- Jain's fairness index drops but total fabric utilisation may not — monitoring that
  watches only aggregate throughput misses it entirely

## 3. The gaps we identified in the original framing

### Gap 1 — Between "new attacker type" and "new place"
The original framing said the attacker is new because it is precise rather than
volumetric. But it did not show why precision is *necessary or advantageous* over
volume in this specific environment. A reviewer will ask: why can't a standard flood
also saturate a single link? It can. So the "new attacker" claim was floating without
load-bearing support.

**How to close it:** Show that the precision attack achieves saturation while
staying below rate-limiting and throttling thresholds that successfully stop a
volumetric flood in the same topology. This makes "new attacker type" a specific
empirical claim, not a description.

### Gap 2 — Between "new place" and "new solution"
The study identified ECMP in SDN as a new target environment. But the solution —
use CSPRNG — does not depend on anything specific about SDN or ECMP. The same fix
applies to HashDoS in a web server. So the "new place" and the "new solution" were
not actually connected. The target was new but the solution was generic.

**How to close it:** Find and demonstrate something specific about SDN/ECMP that
changes the solution space. The candidate: in SDN the salt is controller-managed,
meaning a single misconfigured or compromised controller propagates a weak salt to
the entire fabric simultaneously — a single point of failure that does not exist in
per-device software hash maps. Salt rotation at the controller level is therefore
a fabric-wide fix, not a per-device one. That is an ECMP-specific property.

### Gap 3 — The QRNG gap (runs across all three claims)
QRNG appeared in the study as part of a "quantum" framing but:
- QRNG is not needed to defeat the attacker — CSPRNG does it equally
- QRNG does not change the attack surface
- QRNG does not extend the attacker effort curve in any interesting way

QRNG was present in the experiment table without adding anything to the findings.
A reviewer who notices this will question whether the quantum framing is motivated
reasoning.

**How to close it:** Reframe QRNG as a calibration ceiling, not a solution.
It shows what happens when the salt is provably non-reconstructable by any means.
The null result — QRNG buys nothing over CSPRNG in this threat model — is itself
a finding worth stating explicitly. Honest null results are well regarded in
measurement papers.

## 4. The new attacker framing — and why it holds

### The specific claim
This attacker is not just "precise instead of volumetric." It is specifically
shaped to be invisible to the two defences that stop every other DoS attacker:
**rate limiting and throttling.**

**Rate limiting fails** because the precision attacker does not need to send a lot
of traffic. No individual source exceeds any bandwidth threshold. The rate limiter
never fires. The damage is done by placement, not quantity.

**Throttling fails** because the attacker varies their 5-tuples deliberately —
many different source and destination combinations, all crafted to hash to the
same bucket. From the throttle's perspective this looks like organic traffic
diversity from many different sources. There is no single source or flow to
throttle.

The attacker is structurally immune to source-based detection because there is
no anomalous source. The damage is mathematical, not behavioural. This is a new
attacker class in a precise sense: one that occupies the gap between rate limiting
and throttling, the two tools that define current DoS defence practice.

### Why the solution is categorically different
Salt rotation with a CSPRNG or QRNG source is not a better rate limiter or a
smarter throttle. It operates at a completely different layer:

- Rate limiting and throttling act on **traffic behaviour** — they watch what
  flows are doing and respond after the fact
- Salt rotation acts on **the hash function itself** — it changes the mapping
  that the attacker's pre-computed collisions depend on, before any traffic
  is evaluated

A packet crafted to collide on link 2 under salt A lands on link 3 under salt B.
The attacker's work is invalidated at the moment of rotation, not detected and
blocked. This means the defence has no false-positive problem and no detection
latency — there is nothing to detect; the attack simply stops working.

**Rotation frequency is the tunable parameter.** The attacker needs time to
reconstruct the new salt and recompute a collision set. If rotation happens faster
than that reconstruction window, the attack cannot sustain saturation regardless
of attacker knowledge level. This gives operators a concrete, measurable knob tied
directly to the attacker's computational constraints.

## 5. The five experiments — what each proves

All five experiments are designed to run on the simulated Mininet/OVS topology.
They are mechanism experiments, not scale experiments. The questions they answer
are yes/no or ratio questions — none require 10G links or real ASIC hardware.

The one scale caveat to state honestly in the paper: absolute time-to-saturation
numbers will be lower on real 10G hardware than on a simulated gigabit topology.
But the relative numbers — precision attacker versus volumetric, weak PRNG versus
CSPRNG rotation — are scale-invariant ratios. Those are the findings.

---

### Experiment 1 — Baseline: standard defences work against a volumetric attacker

**Purpose:** Control condition. Prove that rate limiting and throttling are correctly
configured and effective against a naive flood. Without this, a reviewer cannot
distinguish "our attacker evades defences" from "the defences were misconfigured."

**Setup:** Enable per-source rate limiting and per-source connection throttling on
the OVS/Ryu topology. Launch a naive volumetric flood — same source IP, no 5-tuple
variation, high packet rate — toward the victim.

**Expected result:** The flood is stopped or heavily degraded. Link utilisation does
not reach saturation. Victim throughput is protected.

**What this proves:** The defences are real and working. Their subsequent failure
against the precision attacker is meaningful, not a setup artefact.

---

### Experiment 2 — The precision attacker evades rate limiting

**Purpose:** Show that the collision-based attacker causes link saturation while
staying below the rate-limiting threshold on every individual source.

**Setup:** Same rate limiting active as Experiment 1. Launch the full-knowledge
precision attacker (salt known, 5-tuples crafted to collide on target link) with
per-source traffic capped at the same threshold — but spread across multiple
attacker sources, each individually compliant.

**Expected result:** Target link reaches saturation. Victim throughput collapses.
No individual source exceeds the rate limit. The rate limiter does not fire on
any source.

**What this proves:** Rate limiting is structurally insufficient against this
attacker. The defence cannot be tuned to catch it without also blocking legitimate
traffic, because there is no per-source anomaly to catch.

---

### Experiment 3 — The precision attacker evades throttling

**Purpose:** Show that the collision-based attacker causes link saturation while
no individual flow triggers throttling rules.

**Setup:** Same throttling active as Experiment 1. Launch the full-knowledge
attacker with 5-tuples varied across many source ports and destination combinations
— all crafted to collide on the target link, but each individually a distinct,
valid-looking flow.

**Expected result:** Target link reaches saturation. Victim throughput collapses.
No individual flow crosses the throttling threshold. The throttle does not fire
on any flow.

**What this proves:** Throttling is structurally insufficient for the same reason
as rate limiting — there is no anomalous source or flow to throttle.

---

### Experiment 4 — Salt rotation defeats the attacker

**Purpose:** Show that salt rotation is sufficient on its own to defeat the
precision attacker, and does so with zero cost to legitimate traffic.

**Setup:** Run the full-knowledge attacker against three configurations in sequence:
- No rotation, weak PRNG salt (attack succeeds — replicates original result)
- Rotation active, CSPRNG source (attack fails)
- Rotation active, QRNG source (attack fails — same outcome, calibration ceiling)

Measure Jain's fairness index and victim throughput in each configuration under
both attack traffic and clean background traffic.

**Expected result:** Under weak PRNG, saturation occurs quickly. Under CSPRNG/QRNG
rotation, links stay fair and victim throughput is unaffected regardless of how
long the attacker runs. Under background traffic only, all three configurations
produce identical fair distribution — rotation has no cost in the non-attack case.

**What this proves:** Salt rotation defeats the attacker at the mechanism level,
not the behaviour level. The defence is also operationally neutral — it does not
degrade legitimate traffic. QRNG null result is confirmed and stated explicitly.

---

### Experiment 5 — Rotation frequency curve

**Purpose:** Quantify the relationship between salt rotation frequency and attacker
success. Produces the paper's second key graph and the actionable specification for
practitioners.

**Setup:** Fix the attacker at partial knowledge level (knows algorithm, guesses
seed space). Vary the rotation interval from very slow (minutes) to very fast
(seconds or sub-second). Measure time-to-saturation and packets-needed-to-saturate
at each rotation interval.

**Expected result:** A curve showing that below a threshold rotation frequency the
attacker can sustain saturation; above that threshold the attack fails entirely.
The threshold maps directly to how long it takes the attacker to brute-force the
seed space — derivable analytically and confirmed empirically.

**What this proves:** Operators have a concrete, principled knob. Rotate faster
than your PRNG's seed space can be exhausted in, and the attack fails. The graph
gives a specific number, not a vague recommendation. This is the difference between
"use CSPRNG" (advice) and "rotate faster than N seconds given seed space S"
(specification).

## 6. The web demonstration plan

The web demo has one job: make the logical structure of the five experiments
visible to someone who has not read the paper. It is planned in two tiers.

### Tier A — Browser-only, no backend

A single static HTML page. All attack logic (salt generation, hash function, bucket
selection) runs in the browser in JavaScript functions that mirror the Python
implementation exactly. The JS hash and Python hash are tested against the same
inputs to guarantee they never drift apart. Deployable on GitHub Pages, usable as
a conference poster QR code or paper supplementary site. Can be built immediately.

### Tier B — Connected to the real testbed

The same front-end, but link utilisation bars and victim throughput numbers are
driven by real OpenFlow port-stat counters from OVS, pushed over a WebSocket
from a thin API layer on the Ryu controller. The attacker script is triggered from
the page. Two sub-modes: live (testbed running on a lab machine or cloud VM) and
replay (a recorded sweep of all conditions, played back on demand — no live
infrastructure needed for a conference demo).

### The three scenes the demo shows in sequence

**Scene 1 — Standard defences working**
The topology is visible: attacker, switch, four parallel links, victim. A naive
flood launches. The rate limiter fires visually. The links stay balanced. The victim
throughput bar stays healthy. The viewer understands: standard defences work against
standard attackers. This is Experiment 1 made visible.

**Scene 2 — The precision attacker evading both defences**
The attacker mode switches to precision. The rate limiter indicator stays green.
The throttle indicator stays green. But one link bar climbs steadily to red while
the others stay idle. The victim throughput bar collapses. The viewer sees the gap:
everything looks fine to the defences, but the victim is being hit. The salt value
is shown on screen — a predictable, repeating number under weak PRNG. This covers
Experiments 2 and 3.

**Scene 3 — Salt rotation defeating the attack**
The salt source switches to CSPRNG with rotation active. The same attacker script
runs. The link bars scatter randomly — no single link climbs. A rotation event fires,
the salt value changes to a new opaque number, and the attacker's previously crafted
5-tuples disperse. The victim throughput bar stays healthy. A rotation frequency
slider lets the viewer slow rotation until the attacker re-establishes saturation,
then speed it back up until saturation collapses — the Experiment 5 curve made
interactive. When QRNG is selected the page shows the entropy source provenance
(timestamp, byte count, API endpoint) without overstating the difference from CSPRNG.

### Why the demo is not just supplementary material
The demo is the paper's argument made visual. A reviewer or conference attendee
who watches the three scenes understands the contribution before reading a word of
the paper. The demo also serves as a reproducibility artefact: anyone can verify
the mechanism in a browser without running the full Mininet stack.

## 7. How this strengthens the paper

### What changes structurally, not just in terms of added data

The original 72/100 assessment reflected a study with one clean result but an
incomplete argument. The additions above close the argument:

**The contribution is no longer just "predictable salt is bad."**
It is now: this attack class is structurally immune to the two defences that define
current DoS practice, and here is a mechanism-level defence that closes the gap,
with empirical characterisation of the parameters that matter.

**The attacker is defined by what it evades, not just how it works.**
Experiments 1–3 create the logical structure the paper needs: here is the standard
defence, here is why it fails, here is what does work. That is a complete argument.

**The QRNG framing becomes honest.**
QRNG is no longer presented as necessary. It is a calibration ceiling. The null
result is stated explicitly. Honest null results are well regarded in measurement
papers and pre-empt the reviewer question "why do you need quantum here?"

**The rotation frequency curve is a citable, actionable result.**
A graph giving the exact rotation interval at which the attack fails — anchored to
the attacker's reconstruction time — gives practitioners something to implement.

**The paper's identity shifts from:**
> A measurement of ECMP hash-salt predictability as an attack vector

**To:**
> A characterisation of a new attacker class that occupies the gap between rate
> limiting and throttling, with an empirical demonstration of a mechanism-level
> defence and a quantified rotation-frequency specification for practitioners

That is a stronger venue pitch, a cleaner related-work position, and a more
defensible contribution statement. The simulated topology is sufficient to support
all of it.

## 8. Key decisions and their reasoning — quick reference

| Decision | Reasoning |
|---|---|
| Frame as attack paper, not quantum paper | CSPRNG alone defeats the attack; QRNG framing is not defensible |
| QRNG as calibration ceiling, not solution | Honest null result; pre-empts reviewer objection |
| Add rate-limit and throttle experiments | Closes gap between "new attacker" claim and evidence |
| Simulated topology is sufficient | All experiments are mechanism questions, not scale questions |
| Rotation frequency as the key parameter | Turns "use CSPRNG" advice into a practitioner specification |
| Web demo in three scenes | Makes the logical argument visible before paper is read |
| Tier A demo first, Tier B second | Tier A usable immediately; Tier B depends on testbed being instrumented |
| JS and Python hash functions tested together | Prevents demo from silently drifting from real implementation |
| Multi-tenant cloud as primary scenario | Most plausible threat model; strongest venue pitch |

## 9. Open questions at the end of this conversation

- Does anything specific about SDN controller-managed salts (single point of
  failure for the whole fabric) change the solution space in a way worth measuring?
- Should the related work section be planned before or after the experiments are run?
- What is the right rotation frequency unit — per-flow, per-epoch, per-time-interval?
- Is the partial knowledge level (knows algorithm, guesses seed space) sufficiently
  distinct from blind to justify a separate experiment condition, or should it be
  collapsed with full knowledge for the paper?
- Does the replay dataset need to cover all 3 × 3 conditions (three salt sources ×
  three knowledge levels) or is a subset sufficient for the demo?
